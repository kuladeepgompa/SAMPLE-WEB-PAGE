SDG 14: Life Below Water

Conserve and sustainably use the oceans, seas, and marine resources for sustainable development

Introduction

The ocean drives global systems that make the Earth habitable for humankind. Our rainwater, drinking water, weather, climate, coastlines, much of our food, and even the oxygen in the air we breathe are all ultimately provided and regulated by the sea. Despite this, oceans are under significant threat due to human activities.

From pollution to overfishing and climate change, the challenges facing our oceans are immense, but so are the opportunities to protect and restore them. The Sustainable Development Goal 14 (SDG 14) aims to "Conserve and sustainably use the oceans, seas and marine resources for sustainable development."

Key Facts

Fact 1

Oceans cover more than 70% of the Earth’s surface, and are critical to regulating the climate, absorbing CO2, and providing resources for life on Earth.

Fact 2

Over 3 billion people depend on marine and coastal biodiversity for their livelihoods, with the fishing industry alone employing millions globally.

Fact 3

Oceans absorb about 30% of the carbon dioxide produced by humans, buffering the impacts of global warming, but this also contributes to ocean acidification, threatening marine ecosystems.

Fact 4

More than 8 million tons of plastic enter the oceans every year, leading to severe consequences for marine life, including entanglement, ingestion, and toxic chemical pollution.

Challenges

  • Pollution, particularly from plastics and chemicals, severely impacts marine life and ecosystems. Microplastics have been found in the deepest parts of the ocean, affecting even the smallest organisms at the base of the marine food chain.
  • Overfishing leads to the depletion of fish stocks and disrupts marine biodiversity. About 33% of global fish stocks are overexploited, pushing some species to the brink of extinction.
  • Climate change causes ocean acidification, coral bleaching, and rising sea levels. Coral reefs, which support about 25% of all marine species, are particularly vulnerable, with more than half already experiencing bleaching.
  • Destructive fishing practices such as bottom trawling and the use of explosives not only deplete fish populations but also destroy essential marine habitats like coral reefs and seagrass beds.

Targets

Target 14.1

By 2025, prevent and significantly reduce marine pollution of all kinds, particularly from land-based activities, including marine debris, nutrient pollution, and chemical runoff.

Target 14.2

By 2020, sustainably manage and protect marine and coastal ecosystems to avoid significant adverse impacts, including by strengthening resilience and restoring degraded ecosystems.

Target 14.3

Minimize and address the impacts of ocean acidification, including through enhanced scientific cooperation at all levels.

Target 14.5

By 2020, conserve at least 10% of coastal and marine areas, consistent with national and international law and based on the best available scientific information.

Target 14.6

By 2020, prohibit certain forms of fisheries subsidies which contribute to overcapacity and overfishing, eliminate subsidies that contribute to illegal, unreported and unregulated fishing and refrain from introducing new such subsidies, recognizing that appropriate and effective special and differential treatment for developing and least developed countries should be an integral part of the World Trade Organization fisheries subsidies negotiation

Target 14.7

By 2030, increase the economic benefits to Small Island developing States and least developed countries from the sustainable use of marine resources, including through sustainable management of fisheries, aquaculture and tourism

Target 14.a

Increase scientific knowledge, develop research capacity and transfer marine technology, taking into account the Intergovernmental Oceanographic Commission Criteria and Guidelines on the Transfer of Marine Technology, in order to improve ocean health and to enhance the contribution of marine biodiversity to the development of developing countries, in particular small island developing States and least developed countries

Target 14.b

Provide access for small-scale artisanal fishers to marine resources and markets

Target 14.c

Enhance the conservation and sustainable use of oceans and their resources by implementing international law as reflected in United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, which provides the legal framework for the conservation and sustainable use of oceans and their resources, as recalled in paragraph 158 of "The future we want"

Actions

There are many actions we can take to support SDG 14, from individual efforts to policy changes. Examples include:

  • Reducing plastic use by opting for reusable items and participating in local beach cleanup efforts to help prevent marine debris.
  • Supporting sustainable seafood consumption by choosing certified products that follow eco-friendly fishing practices.
  • Advocating for stronger ocean protection laws and international agreements, such as the banning of harmful fishing subsidies that contribute to overfishing.
  • Backing research and innovation aimed at creating solutions for marine conservation, such as biodegradable materials and marine habitat restoration techniques.

Success Stories

Story 1: The Great Barrier Reef

Through coordinated conservation efforts, parts of the Great Barrier Reef have seen a reduction in coral bleaching, and ongoing restoration programs are bringing back marine biodiversity in several areas.

Story 2: Plastic Bans

Countries like Kenya, India, and the European Union have implemented strict bans on single-use plastic bags, significantly reducing plastic waste in both terrestrial and marine environments. These bans have inspired other nations to follow suit.

Story 3: Marine Protected Areas (MPAs)

Global efforts to expand Marine Protected Areas have shown positive results. Chile, for example, created one of the largest MPAs in the world, preserving over 1 million square kilometers of ocean territory.
